    Key rates in this scenario

      Sources

        • Annual projection rows; retirement and pension starts use whole ages.
        • Age Pension estimate assumes a homeowner couple and excludes the principal residence.
        • Share values remain flat until sale.
        • Capital-loss carry-forward is not modelled.
        • Survival is illustrative and Person 1-specific.
        • Other income is CPI-indexed and split 50/50 for tax when marked taxable. Other assets count in the Age Pension assets test but are not deemed, and no CGT is modelled on their disposal.
        • Early super access is simplified, not full Transition-to-Retirement (TTR). There is no 10%-of-balance annual payment cap, no commutation restriction, and no automatic cessation rule. Tax treatment assumes the member is over 60, where TTR and full retirement-phase withdrawals are both tax-free; the model will misstate results for anyone under 60 drawing super while still working.
        How each projected year is funded, in order
        • Guaranteed income first: salary, defined-benefit or UK pensions, and the Aust. Age Pension are counted before anything is drawn.
        • ABP minimum drawdown: when enabled, the legislated minimum comes out of each account-based pension regardless of need.
        • Configured drawdown tiers: any remaining gap up to the Preferred Retirement Income is filled tier by tier until the target is met or every tier is exhausted.
        • Surplus is banked: income above the Essential Annual Budget moves to savings and earns taxable interest in later years.
        • Shortfall: flagged only when every tier is exhausted and income remains below the Essential Annual Budget.
        • A two-source tier uses the chosen split while both sources remain available.
        How each year is calculated (step by step)

        Every projection year runs this exact sequence, in nominal (future) dollars. The table and chart then divide by the cumulative inflation factor to show today’s dollars, unless the table display is switched to Nominal.

        1. Super access. If a person reaches their super access age this year, the selected percentage of their whole balance moves to the ABP (retirement/drawdown) phase. This happens before growth.
        2. Salary. If still working: salary × inflation factor × optional compounded growth above inflation (0% by default), plus SG contributions (salary × SG% × 0.85 after contributions tax) into accumulation super.
        3. Investment growth. The full year’s estimated net return after fees and tax is applied: accumulation balance × (1 + accumulation return), ABP balance × (1 + retirement return). A 6% nominal return with 2.5% inflation is about a 3.4% real return, which is why balances shown in today’s dollars grow more slowly than the headline rate.
        4. ABP minimum drawdown. If enabled, the legislated minimum is taken from the post-growth ABP balance: 4% under 65, 5% at 65–74, 6% at 75–79, 7% at 80–84, 9% at 85–89, 11% at 90–94, 14% at 95+.
        5. Top-up draws. If guaranteed income (work, UK/DB pensions, other income, Age Pension) plus the ABP minimums fall short of the Preferred Retirement Income, the gap is drawn from the drawdown priority tiers. Spending is capped at the Essential Annual Budget; income above it is banked to savings.
        6. Display conversion. Each row divides nominal values by the cumulative inflation factor for that year (today’s dollars).

        Table columns. Super balance columns are end-of-year balances after growth and draws. “UK State Pension (gross)” is the gross pension (frozen pensions stay fixed in nominal GBP, so their today’s-dollars value falls each year). “ABP min draw” shows the mandatory minimum with the applied rate; “Top-up draw” is the extra super drawn to reach the Preferred Retirement Income. “Tax” is total household income tax including Medicare, LITO/SAPTO and the UPP deduction on UK pensions. “After tax” is total household after-tax income; “Banked” is the part of it above the Essential Annual Budget, added to savings.
